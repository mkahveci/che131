Introduction

When carrying out laboratory procedures, a variety of glassware can be used. Determining the appropriate type of glassware to use relies on an understanding of the accuracy and precision required for the experiment, and then knowing which glassware best fits these needs. By determining the density of a liquid using various glassware types and comparing the average and standard deviations of the densities determined, the relative accuracy and precision of a graduated cylinder, volumetric pipette, and burette will be determined. Additionally, different methods of determining the density, both from replicate measurements and from graphical analysis, will be explored.

Physical and Chemical Principles

The density of an object, d, is defined as:

d=\frac{m}{V}\label{eq:density}

where m is the mass of the object and V is its volume. Density is an intensive property of a material. This means that regardless of the sample amount, the density of each sample will always be the same as long as the temperature is the same throughout. Mass, of course, is a direct measure of the amount of material present in an object; it is not sensitive to temperature. For example, a 10 g ice cube will always be 10 g whether it is in the freezer or in a puddle on the floor. Volume, however, is sensitive to temperature. As a result, the density of an object is dependent on temperature.

The volume of an object is a measurement of how much space the object occupies. Volume is a key measurement in the chemistry laboratory, and as a result, chemists generally try to measure it with as much accuracy and precision as possible. In order to do this, a chemist will use special glassware in the laboratory to measure liquids. Glassware used in volume measurements is often referred to as volumetric glassware. Such glassware is mass-produced under strict quality control and is available from many different laboratory supply companies.

The glassware supplier guarantees that the equipment will either deliver or contain the volume specified on the body of the device to within a known tolerance or uncertainty range. This is sometimes printed right on the glassware. Glassware used to transfer liquids from one place to another are often designated to deliver (TD), whereas those designed to contain liquids are marked with the code TC. The burette (Figure 1) and volumetric pipette (Figure 2, right) are marked TD. Volumetric flasks (figure 3, left) and graduated cylinders belong in the latter class. “To Deliver” glassware may retain a small amount of liquid in their tips after the liquid is delivered into the container. Never blow or shake this small amount of material out of the device! The glassware is calibrated to account for the liquid that is left behind.

As with any piece of equipment, there is always some user error. To gain a sense of this error, scientists must make replicate measurements. This means that the desired measurement is made three or more times under the same conditions. The observations are then averaged. It is also necessary to report the degree of uncertainty in the measurements by calculating the standard deviation of the group of measurements. Appendix A in the General Chemistry Laboratory Appendices file on D2L provides a thorough background on the concept of averages and standard deviations.
